# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Active job seekers (developers, engineers, professionals) in DE/US/CA/UK markets who are fatigued by job board noise.
They want passive, automated monitoring: set it once, get notified only when something matches. Context: at their desk
or phone, likely mid-job-search, somewhat cynical about spam and recruiter churn.

## Product Purpose

Your Job Finder aggregates jobs from major platforms, runs AI matching against the subscriber's profile, and sends a
single curated daily email only when there's a genuine match. Success looks like: user subscribes in under 2 minutes,
trusts that they won't be spammed, and eventually gets a job through a match they wouldn't have found manually.

## Brand Personality

Precise, edgy, confident. Three words: sharp, anti-noise, decisive. The brand should feel like it has an opinion. Not
warm startup, not corporate job board. Closer to a tool built by engineers who were tired of the garbage fire that is
modern job searching.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS cream: gradient blobs, testimonial carousels, social proof grids, "the future of X" copy
- Job board corporate: LinkedIn/Indeed visual language, blue-and-white grids, recruiter-facing UX
- Hacker/neon dark: terminal aesthetic, neon on black, "we're so technical" posturing

## Design Principles

1. **Anti-noise as visual language.** Every element removed is a signal that this product filters. Restraint is the
   message.
2. **Earned trust, not performed trust.** No trust badges unless they're genuinely earned. Privacy claims show up in
   copy, not shield icons.
3. **Edge with legibility.** Edgier doesn't mean unreadable. Sharp typographic choices and strong contrast must still
   serve comprehension first.
4. **Tools, not toys.** References like Linear: precise grid, strong density, purposeful color. Not playful, not
   decorative for its own sake.
5. **Opinionated defaults.** The design makes choices so users don't have to. Forms are pre-scoped. CTAs are singular.
   No "explore all options" indirection.

## Tone

- Copy is terse and direct. No filler words.
- Never explain what the product does in terms of features. Explain it in terms of the user's problem solved.
- Avoid: "cutting-edge", "powerful", "seamless", "revolutionary", "game-changing"
- Avoid: exclamation marks in body copy
- Avoid: second-guessing the user ("you might want to...", "feel free to...")
